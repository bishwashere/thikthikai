One other paper published in the of Archives of Neurology, conducted a study with 12 patients with clinical EEG manifestations reminiscent of temporal lobe epilepsy reported symptoms of dissociative states. This paper basically suggested that in the observed cases dissociative states may constitute complex behavioural manifestations of chronic limbic epilepsy. Apart from all the technical jargon, the paper records two cases: 1) a 26-year-old, righthanded Roman Catholic woman. She reports multiple episodes of altered consciousness associated with lip smacking and rocking movements. There are also panic episodes, auditory and visual hallucinations, deja vu, and depersonalization. The patient stated her conviction that she is possessed by the devil. She feels that there is a demon in her body who cackles inside her head. She hears his voice telling her to do horrible things, ranging from throwing cups of coffee at other people's faces to killing herself. She was subsequently taken through the rite of exorcism. The priest then announced that she had been exorcised. She is now attending extension courses at the college level and obtaining mostly As. What's interesting in this case the neurological data that was extracted from the patient. The EEG showed anterior bitemporal spikes. Although the CT scan and results of neurological examination are within normal limits. Because of the suggested neurological connection, the supposed supernatural phenomenon of demonic possession can be reduced to neuroscience and explained in a naturalistic way [6].

There is a certain appeal in explaining the phenomenon of demonic possession as a special type DID (DTD- Dissociative Trance disorder or PTD - Possession Trance disorder). There are also cases out there that are assertively believed to be instances of demonic possession but are later shown otherwise.

A 38-year-old, righthanded Roman Catholic man who had no prior history of neurological or psychiatric impairment. Who was operated on to remove an abscess (a localized collection of pus). Two years after surgery, he had a grand mal seizure that prompted hospitalization. He was not unusually religious, but become preoccupied with religion after surgery and started to write excessively even though he was virtually illiterate. The patient kept voluminous notes concerning his spells in which his beliefs of the devil trying to get inside was expressed. The patient repeated his conviction that the devil was actually inside his body. As proof for this demonic possession, he related frightening nightmares and thoughts of raping his own daughters. He kept repeating that there are "demons within me."

The EEG showed a sharp wave and spike focus mostly on the right mid-temporal area. The CT scan showed an area of low density in the right parietooccipital region. Neurological examination showed sensory extinction on the left. The patient did very well after receiving proper medication. The various manifestations disappeared and he stopped believing that he was possessed [6].

A 22-year-old unemployed Hindu Indian male, an aunt, jealous of the success of the patient's family, fed him and his elder brother cursed sweet rice, rendering them susceptible to spirit possession. His family had sent him to holy places in India where he was exorcised, by a Hindu priest and later a Moslem peer. Impatient with the failure, which seemed to increase the ghost's anger, he unsuccessfully consulted Christian priests. Routine tests revealed that the patient was normal. These tests included EEG and tomography. The patient was diagnosed with Dissociative sate or paranoid schizophrenia. Exorcism having failed, he was prescribed relevant medication (trifluoperazine (4mg daily)) producing apparent remission and remaining in remission 12 weeks after [7].

There is also one very interesting case that is worth accounting for - the exorcism of Martha Brossier. Martha Brossier was a celebrity in France in the 1590s. She was a woman possessed by demons and her family took "the act" on tour. They went from town-to-town showing off the Satanic entity that resided within their child's soul, all before a packed crowd. It caught so much attention that King Henri IV himself ordered her exorcism. Martha Brossier was brought to the Abbey of St. Geneuesua, to be exorcised, under Marescot's watch, by the Bishop of Paris himself. He had some of the highest priests in the country gather around her, sprinkling her with holy water and reciting scriptures in Latin while the demon in Brossier, tormented by the Holy Scriptures, screamed in agony and pain. Michel Marescot, the personal physician to King Henri IV, was given the task of exposing her. Marthe Brossier was brought to the Abbey of St. Geneuesua, to be exorcised, under Marescot's watch, by the Bishop of Paris himself. Nearly as soon as Brossier kneeled down with the bishop to pray, the demon took hold of her. She fell onto her back, convulsing and breathing like a wild animal. Her eyes turned back in her head, her tongue flared out, and a dark, gravelly voice from within her stomach shouted vulgar, unprintable words. But what Brossier didn't know was that her exorcism was a sham. The holy water was just ordinary water, and the Latin books the priests were reading were nothing more than an old poem by Virgil [17]. It can be understood that the ritual of exorcism acted as a suggestive situation for her to play out her so-called demonic entity. But since the ritual itself was a scam, we can safely infer that the demons are a scam and hence, unreal as well.

A similar view on the phenomenon of demonic possession is held by Christopher Charles French is a British psychologist specialising in the psychology of paranormal beliefs and experiences, cognition and emotion (a debunker). Possession is categorised into Central possession: The person possessed is a respected member of the society and the possession spirit is usually a deity and the performance serve to reinforce the central values of the society. Butha Kola in Coastal Karnataka is a good example of central possession.

In peripheral possession: The person possessed person is a socially marginal and powerless member of the society. The possessing spirit is usually a capricious and amoral. The performance is typically associated with illness and distress on the part of the possessed. Chris French further argues that whole ritual has social significance; since the exorcists are seen as powerful people who fight against evil. Hence their role in the society gets emphasised and for the possessed, the ritual is a platform to enact their otherwise socially tabooed or unaccepted desires [18].

A philosophical Approach:

Vedantic Indian philosophy argues for the Brahaman (God) and a part of it which is called the Athman (soul). The debate whether the Brahaman is identical in all aspects to the Athman gives rise to two schools of Indian philosophy called Dvaitha and Advaitha. The former school is a dualist, which argues that the athman is of a lesser degree than the Brahaman and the latter argues that the Brahaman and the athman are identical in all regard. Now, given this understanding of the Vedantic philosophy, a question arises on how a body which contains the Brahaman can be occupied by a demonic entity? The simple answer is NO; it cannot occupy. And this reasoning is consistent with the categories these philosophers make about the human body. The human body has a material body, a soul, a mind and also the self. Hence demonic possession can be understood as an intrusion or alteration of the self or the mind [19]. Coincidentally, this reasoning is consistent with the findings of modern psychiatry and psychology.

In Western philosophy, when there are two contending theories that explain the same phenomena, we a principle called The Occam's Razor which is a guiding tool in choosing the right theory. Basically, every theory has certain predicates that are irreducible [22]. For example: All fundamental particles in physics are irreducible. While theorizing certain irreducible are formed from observation and some are posited to explain a phenomenon. For example, in J J Thompson's Experiment, to explain the phenomenon in his experiment, a negatively charged electron was posited. Now, Occam's Razor says that while choosing between contending theories, we choose the simplest one; meaning, a theory with lesser number of irreducible. Debunkers knowingly or unknowingly use Occam's Razor in their analysis of the supernatural. Ouija boards are debunked with the help of Occam's Razor. Basically, through controlled experiments, it is shown that the game can be explained with Naturalism (that is, with all that we know about science and psychology). Finally, Occam's Razor is employed to eliminate the spirit entity that people usually believe moves the dial of the Ouija board. In simple terms, when all the observed phenomenon can be explained with Naturalist theory, then why posit a supernatural entity to explain the same phenomenon? [21]
